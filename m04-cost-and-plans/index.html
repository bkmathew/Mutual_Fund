    M4 · Cost & Plan Choice¶

    Learning objectives

    By the end of this module you will be able to:

    • Explain what the Total Expense Ratio (TER) is, how it is deducted invisibly inside the NAV, and why cost is "the only certainty".
    • Read the 2026 unbundled structure — TER = BER + brokerage + statutory/regulatory levies — and the new BER caps and brokerage caps.
    • Quantify the regular-vs-direct gap and its 20-year compounding cost in rupees.
    • Understand the counter-intuitive nuance: that unbundling has in several cases raised the reported TER (especially in direct plans) — and what that does and does not mean for what you actually pay.

    Builds on M1 (NAV is struck after expenses) and M2 (who is paid — AMC, distributor). This is the most decision-relevant beginner module: over a lifetime, cost is the variable you control most and the market controls least.

    1. Intuition first — why cost is "the only certainty"¶

    You cannot know what return a fund will deliver. You can know, almost to the rupee, what it will charge. Returns are uncertain; costs are contractual. And because costs are deducted every single day, they compound against you exactly as returns compound for you. A 1%-a-year cost difference sounds trivial. Over an investing lifetime it is not — it can quietly consume a sixth of your final wealth, as §5 shows.

    This is why the legendary indexer John Bogle called costs "the tyranny of compounding costs" — the mirror image of the miracle of compounding returns.

    2. What the TER is, and how you pay it without seeing a bill¶

    Definition — TER (Total Expense Ratio)

    The TER is the annual running cost of a scheme, expressed as a percentage of its assets. It bundles the AMC's management fee, administration, registrar and (in regular plans) distributor commission, and other allowable costs. It is accrued daily and subtracted before the NAV is struck — so you never receive an invoice; you simply earn a slightly lower NAV. (Recall from M1: expenses are a liability subtracted in the NAV formula.)

    Worked example 1 — the invisible deduction¶

    You hold ₹5,00,000 in a fund with a TER of 1.50%. Over a year, roughly:

    \[\text{Annual cost} = ₹5{,}00{,}000 \times 1.50\% = ₹7{,}500\]

    That ₹7,500 is never billed — it is shaved off the NAV in tiny daily slivers (about ₹20.5 a day). You feel it only as a return that is 1.50% lower than the fund's gross performance.

    3. The 2026 overhaul — TER gets "unbundled"¶

    The single biggest 2026 change is structural. The old model was one all-in TER cap. The new model unbundles the cost into clearly separated, separately disclosed parts:

    \[\text{TER} \;=\; \underbrace{\text{BER}}_{\text{fund-management fee, capped}} \;+\; \underbrace{\text{Brokerage \& transaction}}_{\text{capped, on actuals}} \;+\; \underbrace{\text{Statutory \& regulatory levies}}_{\text{GST, STT, stamp duty, CTT, exchange/SEBI fees}}\]

    Definition — BER (Base Expense Ratio)

    The BER reflects only the fund-management expense the AMC charges. It is the part SEBI caps. Brokerage and unavoidable government/market levies now sit on top of the BER, charged on actuals and shown separately, rather than being squeezed inside a single cap.

    3.1 The new BER caps (lower than the old all-in caps)¶

    Scheme type (open-ended) Old highest TER cap 2026 highest BER cap
    Equity, AUM < ₹500 cr 2.25% 2.10%
    Debt, AUM < ₹500 cr 2.00% 1.85%
    Index funds & ETFs 1.00% 0.90%
    Close-ended equity 1.25% 1.00%

    As with the old slabs, the cap steps down as AUM grows (a larger fund must charge a lower percentage), preserving the economies-of-scale principle. [verify full slab table & section no.]

    3.2 The corrected brokerage caps¶

    Seed-document correction

    The project's seed document quoted the wrong brokerage figures. The verified 2026 caps are:

    Transaction Old cap 2026 cap
    Cash-market 12 bps (0.12%) 6 bps (0.06%)
    Derivatives 5 bps (0.05%) 2 bps (0.02%)
    Extra allowance for exit-load schemes 5 bps (0.05%) removed

    These are independently confirmed against SEBI and multiple industry sources (see Sources). Use these figures, not the seed's.

    3.3 Statutory levies "on actuals"¶

    GST (on the management fee and brokerage), STT, stamp duty, CTT and exchange/SEBI fees are now charged at the exact amount incurred, on top of the BER, and disclosed separately — rather than being absorbed inside a single cap as before.

    4. The counter-intuitive nuance — why "transparency" can raise the reported TER¶

    Here is the subtlety the headlines miss, and that this program insists you understand. SEBI lowered the BER cap — yet the reported TER of several schemes went up after 1 April 2026, most visibly in direct plans.

    How can lowering the cap raise the number? Because costs that were previously hidden or absorbed (brokerage, GST, transaction levies) are now itemised and added on top of the BER inside the disclosed TER. The number you see rose largely because it now shows more of what was always being paid — a reclassification into daylight, not necessarily a fresh increase in what leaves your pocket.

    A reported case (illustrative, from AMFI data)

    Industry data after the rule change showed some direct-plan schemes whose reported expense jumped sharply — in one cited instance from about 0.39% (≈ ₹3,900 a year on ₹10 lakh) to 1.59% (≈ ₹15,900 a year). Much of that leap reflects previously-unshown transaction costs now surfaced into the headline TER, plus transition-period optics. Read a 2026 TER change as a question to investigate ("what's now included?"), not an automatic signal that the fund got dearer.

    What to actually do

    Compare funds on like-for-like, post-unbundling TER, and where possible on BER plus typical transaction costs together. Don't compare a fund's pre-April-2026 TER with its post-April figure and conclude it "got expensive" — you may be comparing a partial number with a fuller one.

    5. Regular vs Direct — the choice that costs the most¶

    Every scheme comes in two plans:

    • Regular plan — bought through a distributor (MFD/bank/some platforms); a commission (trail) is embedded in the TER and paid to the distributor out of the fund.
    • Direct plan — bought straight from the AMC (or an execution-only/RIA platform); no distributor commission, so the TER is lower — typically by 0.5% to 1.0% a year.

    The two plans hold the identical portfolio and the same fund manager; they differ only in cost. That cost gap compounds.

    Worked example 2 — the 20-year gap in rupees¶

    Invest a lump sum of ₹10,00,000 for 20 years. Assume a 12% gross return. Direct TER 0.60% → net 11.4%; Regular TER 1.60% → net 10.4% (a 1.0% gap):

    \[\text{Direct} = 10{,}00{,}000 \times 1.114^{20} = ₹86.6\ \text{lakh}$$ $$\text{Regular} = 10{,}00{,}000 \times 1.104^{20} = ₹72.3\ \text{lakh}\]
    \[\textbf{Cost of the regular plan} = ₹86.6L - ₹72.3L = ₹14.3\ \text{lakh}\]

    A 1%-a-year difference quietly took ₹14.3 lakh — more than the original investment — over 20 years, for the same fund.

    Figure: the same ₹10 lakh at 12% gross, under a 0.60% vs 1.60% TER. The shaded wedge is money lost purely to the higher plan's cost. Illustrative; figures move with actual returns and TERs.

    But 'direct' is not free — it forgoes advice

    Direct saves the commission, not the AMC fee — the AMC still manages the fund. And you give up the distributor's hand-holding. A good adviser who keeps you invested through a crash can be worth far more than 1% a year; a poor one is not. The honest framing: pay for advice if and when it adds value, but never pay a distribution commission for advice you aren't getting. This is where an RIA (fee-only) can beat an MFD (commission) — see M2.

    6. Two more cost levers: performance fees and exit loads¶

    Performance-linked fees (new, optional in 2026). The 2026 framework permits schemes to offer an optional performance-linked fee structure (a lower base fee plus a share of returns above a hurdle), as an alternative to a flat fee. It is opt-in and must be clearly disclosed. [verify section no.]

    Exit load. A charge for redeeming too soon (e.g. 1% if you exit an equity fund within 12 months), meant to deter quick flips and protect remaining unit-holders. It is not a regular cost if you hold for the stated period. Note the 2026 removal of the 5 bps TER allowance that schemes with exit loads used to get — a separate, AMC-side change.

    7. Common mistakes & Do's and Don'ts¶

    Cost traps

    1. "1% is small." Over 20 years a 1% gap can cost more than your entire initial investment (Example 2).
    2. "The TER number went up, so the fund got expensive." Post-2026 it may simply show more of what was always paid (unbundling). Investigate before concluding.
    3. "Direct is always best." Direct is cheaper, but if you need advice to stay the course, fee-only advice can be worth more than its cost. Don't conflate cheap with right.
    4. "A high-AUM fund must be cheap." Caps step down with size, but always check the actual TER — funds price differently within the cap.

    Do

    • Do compare TER first among similar funds — it is the most reliable predictor you control.
    • Do prefer Direct if you are confident managing your own behaviour, or pay an RIA a transparent fee.
    • Do read the post-unbundling TER as BER + transaction + levies, and compare like with like.

    Don't

    • Don't pay a regular-plan commission for advice you never receive.
    • Don't chase last year's return while ignoring a structurally higher cost.

    8. Applicable SEBI (Mutual Funds) Regulations, 2026¶

    • TER unbundling — TER defined as BER + brokerage/transaction + statutory & regulatory levies, the latter two on actuals and separately disclosed. [verify section no.]
    • BER caps — lowered headline caps (equity 2.25%→2.10%, debt 2.00%→1.85%, index/ETF 1.00%→0.90%, close-ended equity 1.25%→1.00%), stepping down by AUM slab. [verify full table]
    • Brokerage caps — cash 6 bps, derivatives 2 bps; removal of the 5 bps exit-load TER allowance. [verify]
    • Optional performance-linked fees — permitted, with disclosure. [verify]
    • Direct plan mandate — every scheme must offer a commission-free direct plan; regular plans embed distributor commission. [verify]

    (The economics for the AMC, distributor margins and the unbundling rationale are deep-dived in M17 and M18.)

    9. Key takeaways¶

    Key takeaways

    • Cost is the only certainty. TER is accrued daily inside the NAV — no bill, but a permanent drag.
    • 2026 unbundles cost: TER = BER (capped) + brokerage (6/2 bps caps) + levies (on actuals).
    • Reported TER sometimes rose post-2026 — largely reclassification into the open, not always a real increase. Compare like-for-like.
    • Regular vs Direct is the costliest single choice: a 1%/yr gap took ₹14.3 lakh on ₹10 lakh over 20 years.
    • Choose Direct if you can self-manage; otherwise pay fee-only (RIA) advice — never a commission for advice you don't get.

    10. A word from the field¶

    On the arithmetic of costs

    "The miracle of compounding returns is overwhelmed by the tyranny of compounding costs."

    — John C. Bogle, The Little Book of Common Sense Investing. Costs are subtracted with the same relentless compounding that grows returns — which is why, of everything an investor can control, minimising cost is the surest edge.
