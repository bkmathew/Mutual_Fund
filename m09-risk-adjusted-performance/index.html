    M9 · Risk-Adjusted Performance¶

    Learning objectives

    By the end of this module you will be able to:

    • Explain why raw return is misleading and define CAGR, volatility (standard deviation), downside deviation and maximum drawdown.
    • Compute and interpret the four workhorse ratios — Sharpe, Sortino, Treynor, Information Ratio — on Indian figures.
    • Choose the right ratio for the question being asked, and read a risk-return scatter.
    • Avoid the classic misuses (annualisation errors, cross-period and cross-asset comparisons, too-short samples).

    This opens Level 3 (Analyst). It gives the math behind the factsheet ratios introduced in M7, and the risk vocabulary the rest of L3 (M10–M14) relies on. Beta and alpha are used here and built in M10.

    1. Intuition first — return per unit of risk¶

    Two funds each returned 13% over five years. One did it with a smooth ride; the other lurched through a 40% crash on the way. They are not equally good. The second investor took far more risk for the same reward — and was far more likely to panic-sell at the bottom (M6). Professional evaluation therefore never asks "what was the return?" alone; it asks "what was the return per unit of risk taken?" Every ratio in this module is a different answer to that one question.

    2. The raw return — CAGR¶

    Before adjusting for risk, measure return correctly. A multi-year result must be annualised as a compound rate, not a simple average.

    Formula — CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate)

    $\(\text{CAGR} = \left(\frac{\text{Ending value}}{\text{Beginning value}}\right)^{1/n} - 1\)$ where n = number of years.

    Worked example 1 — CAGR¶

    ₹1,00,000 grows to ₹2,01,000 in 6 years: $\(\text{CAGR} = (2.01)^{1/6} - 1 = 1.1234 - 1 = 12.34\%\)$ This single number is comparable across funds and periods — unlike "the fund doubled", which hides how long it took.

    3. Measuring risk — four lenses¶

    Definitions — the risk measures

    • Standard deviation (σ, volatility) — how much returns scatter around their average. Computed from periodic (e.g. monthly) returns and annualised: \(\sigma_{\text{annual}} = \sigma_{\text{monthly}} \times \sqrt{12}\). Higher σ = a bumpier ride.
    • Downside deviation — like σ but counts only returns below a target (often the risk-free rate or zero). It ignores "good" volatility (upside), capturing only the risk you actually dislike.
    • Maximum drawdown — the largest peak-to-trough fall over the period. Answers "what is the worst it put me through?" — the number that predicts panic-selling.
    • Beta (β) — sensitivity to the market: β = 1.1 means the fund tends to move 1.1× the index. Introduced here; derived in M10.

    Each lens matters for a different reason: σ for overall bumpiness, downside deviation for the bad bumpiness, drawdown for survivability, beta for market-linked risk.

    4. The four risk-adjusted ratios¶

    We will use one consistent dataset throughout. Risk-free rate Rf = 6.5% (roughly a short Indian G-sec/T-bill yield).

    Return σ Downside dev Beta vs Benchmark (11.5%) Tracking error
    Fund A 14% 18% 12% 1.10 +2.5% 5%
    Fund B 12% 11% 7% 0.80 +0.5% 3%

    4.1 Sharpe ratio — excess return per unit of total risk¶

    Formula — Sharpe ratio

    $\(\text{Sharpe} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}\)$ Excess return (over the risk-free rate) per unit of total volatility. Higher is better. The single most-used measure.

    \[\text{Sharpe}_A = \frac{14 - 6.5}{18} = 0.42 \qquad \text{Sharpe}_B = \frac{12 - 6.5}{11} = 0.50\]

    Fund B wins despite its lower return — it delivered more reward per unit of risk. This is the module's central lesson in one line: higher return ≠ better fund.

    4.2 Sortino ratio — excess return per unit of downside risk¶

    Formula — Sortino ratio

    $\(\text{Sortino} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\text{Downside deviation}}\)$ Like Sharpe, but penalises only harmful (downside) volatility — fairer to funds whose volatility is mostly upside.

    \[\text{Sortino}_A = \frac{7.5}{12} = 0.63 \qquad \text{Sortino}_B = \frac{5.5}{7} = 0.79\]

    Fund B again leads — its losses, specifically, were better contained.

    4.3 Treynor ratio — excess return per unit of market risk¶

    Formula — Treynor ratio

    $\(\text{Treynor} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\beta_p}\)$ Excess return per unit of beta (market risk only). Appropriate when the fund is one part of an already-diversified portfolio, so only its market risk matters.

    \[\text{Treynor}_A = \frac{7.5}{1.10} = 6.82 \qquad \text{Treynor}_B = \frac{5.5}{0.80} = 6.88\]

    Close, with B marginally ahead.

    4.4 Information Ratio — active skill per unit of active risk¶

    Formula — Information Ratio (IR)

    $\(\text{IR} = \frac{R_p - R_b}{\text{Tracking error}}\)$ Return above the benchmark per unit of tracking error (how much the fund deviates from the benchmark). The cleanest measure of an active manager's skill.

    \[\text{IR}_A = \frac{2.5}{5} = 0.50 \qquad \text{IR}_B = \frac{0.5}{3} = 0.17\]

    The nuance that separates analysts from amateurs

    Sharpe says B; the Information Ratio says A. They answer different questions. Sharpe asks "best reward for total risk?" — B. IR asks "most skill in beating the benchmark?" — A. A fund can be a great standalone holding (high Sharpe) yet a weak active bet (low IR), or vice-versa. Always match the ratio to the decision.

    5. Seeing it — the risk-return scatter¶

    Each fund is a point (risk on the x-axis, return on the y-axis). Draw a line from the risk-free rate to a fund: its slope is the Sharpe ratio. The steeper the line, the more return that fund earned per unit of risk. Fund B's line is steeper than Fund A's even though A is higher up — the visual proof that B is the better risk-adjusted performer. Illustrative figures.

    6. Consistency — rolling returns beat point-to-point¶

    A single CAGR or Sharpe over one window can be a fluke of start/end dates. Rolling returns — recomputing over every possible window (e.g. all 3-year periods, rolled daily) — reveal consistency: the average, the range, and how often the fund beat its benchmark. A fund with a slightly lower average but a much tighter, more reliable rolling distribution is often the better real-world holding, because investors can actually stay the course (M6). Prefer rolling-return analysis for any serious comparison.

    7. Common mistakes & Do's and Don'ts¶

    Misuses of these ratios

    1. Comparing Sharpe across different periods or asset classes. A debt fund's Sharpe and an equity fund's Sharpe over different windows are not comparable.
    2. Annualisation errors. σ scales with √time, returns compound — don't mix monthly and annual figures.
    3. Too-short a sample. A 1-year Sharpe is noise; use 3–5+ years and rolling windows.
    4. Reading Sharpe alone. It penalises upside volatility too — pair it with Sortino and max drawdown.
    5. Ignoring which risk matters. Standalone holding → Sharpe; sleeve of a portfolio → Treynor; active-skill question → IR.

    Do

    • Do annualise consistently and use 3–5+ year rolling windows.
    • Do pick the ratio that matches the decision (total / downside / market / active risk).
    • Do look at max drawdown — the number that predicts whether you'll hold on.

    Don't

    • Don't rank funds on raw return.
    • Don't compare risk-adjusted numbers across mismatched periods or categories.

    9. Key takeaways¶

    Key takeaways

    • Judge funds on return per unit of risk, never raw return.
    • Sharpe (total risk), Sortino (downside), Treynor (market/beta), IR (active skill) — each answers a different question; match the ratio to the decision.
    • In the worked set, Fund B beat Fund A on Sharpe/Sortino/Treynor despite lower return, yet A led on IR — different questions, different winners.
    • On a risk-return chart, Sharpe is the slope from the risk-free rate.
    • Use max drawdown and rolling returns to judge survivability and consistency — not one lucky window.

    10. A word from the field¶

    On what risk really is

    "Risk means more things can happen than will happen."

    — Howard Marks, The Most Important Thing. Risk-adjusted measures exist precisely because the single outcome you observed (a return) was drawn from a range of things that could have happened — and the width of that range, not just the point you landed on, is what you must price.
