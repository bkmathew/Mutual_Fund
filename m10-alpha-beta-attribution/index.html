    M10 · Alpha, Beta, Attribution & Factors¶

    Learning objectives

    By the end of this module you will be able to:

    • Define beta and alpha precisely, estimate them by regression, and use the CAPM line to separate market return from skill.
    • Read R² / correlation and explain why alpha from a low-R² fund is unreliable.
    • Decompose outperformance with Brinson attribution (allocation vs selection vs interaction).
    • Use tracking error and active share to spot closet indexing, and apply the factor lens (size, value, momentum, quality, low-vol) to ask how much "alpha" is really factor exposure.

    Builds directly on M9 (it used beta and alpha; here we derive them). Together M9–M10 are the analyst's measurement core, feeding portfolio internals (M11) and construction (M15–M16).

    1. Intuition first — how much of the return was the market, and how much was skill?¶

    A fund returned 18% in a year the market rose 16%. Did the manager add value? Not necessarily. If the fund simply takes more market risk (a high beta), 18% might be less than its risk entitled it to. To judge a manager you must first strip out the part of the return that came free from the market, and look at what's left. That residue — the return the manager added beyond what market exposure explains — is alpha. Everything in this module is machinery for isolating it.

    2. Beta and the CAPM line¶

    Definition — Beta (β)

    Beta measures a fund's sensitivity to its benchmark. It is the slope when the fund's excess return (over Rf) is regressed on the market's excess return. β = 1 moves with the market; β = 1.2 amplifies it by 20%; β = 0.8 dampens it.

    Formula — CAPM expected return

    E(Rp) = Rf + β (Rm - Rf) The return the fund should earn given its market risk alone. Anything above this line is alpha.

    Worked example 1 — what return did the risk entitle the fund to?¶

    Rf = 6.5%, market Rm = 11.5%, fund β = 1.10: E(Rp) = 6.5 + 1.10 (11.5 - 6.5) = 6.5 + 5.5 = 12.0% The fund's market risk "entitled" it to 12%. If it actually delivered 14%, the extra 2% is alpha (next section). If it delivered only 11%, it destroyed value despite a positive return.

    3. Alpha — the skill residue¶

    Formula — Jensen's alpha

    α = Rp - [Rf + β (Rm - Rf)] Realised return minus the CAPM-expected return. Positive α = return beyond what market risk explains = manager value-add.

    For Example 1: α = 14 − 12 = +2.0%. The manager added two percentage points the market did not hand over for free.

    Seeing beta and alpha together — the regression¶

    When you regress a fund's monthly excess returns on the market's, the slope is beta and the intercept is alpha:

    Each dot is one month. The fitted line's slope (≈1.05) is beta; where it crosses the vertical axis (≈ +0.26%/month ≈ +3.2%/year) is alpha. A manager with skill sits the cloud of points above a pure-beta line. Illustrative data.

    4. R² — is the alpha even trustworthy?¶

    Definition — R² (and correlation)

    R² is the share of a fund's return variance explained by the benchmark (0 to 1). High R² (say 0.90) → the benchmark is the right yardstick and beta/alpha are meaningful. Low R² (say 0.50) → the fund moves to its own drummer, so the regression fit is loose and its alpha is statistically unreliable — a large measured alpha may be noise.

    Don't trust alpha without R²

    A flashy alpha from a fund with R² = 0.5 is largely meaningless — beta is poorly estimated, so the "skill residue" is mostly measurement error. Always read alpha and R² together. (This is also why a sector fund's alpha vs a broad index is suspect — wrong benchmark, low R².)

    5. Attribution — where did the outperformance come from?¶

    Beta/alpha say how much value was added; attribution says from where. The Brinson model splits active return (fund − benchmark) into allocation (over/under-weighting sectors) and selection (picking better stocks within sectors), plus an interaction term.

    Formulas — Brinson attribution (per sector, summed)

    • Allocation = (wp - wb) (Rbsector - Rbtotal)
    • Selection = wb (Rpsector - Rbsector)
    • Interaction = (wp - wb) (Rpsector - Rbsector)

    Worked example 2 — decomposing a manager's edge¶

    A fund vs a two-sector benchmark. Benchmark total return = 0.5×10 + 0.5×14 = 12%.

    Sector Bench wt Fund wt Bench return Fund return
    Financials 50% 60% 10% 12%
    IT 50% 40% 14% 13%

    Fund total = 0.6×12 + 0.4×13 = 12.4%, so active return = +0.4%. Decomposing:

    • Allocation: Fin (0.10)(10−12) = −0.2%; IT (−0.10)(14−12) = −0.2% → −0.4% (over-weighting the lower-returning sector hurt).
    • Selection: Fin 0.5(12−10) = +1.0%; IT 0.5(13−14) = −0.5% → +0.5% (good Financials picks).
    • Interaction: Fin (0.10)(2) + IT (−0.10)(−1) = +0.3%.
    • Check: −0.4 + 0.5 + 0.3 = +0.4% ✓

    Reading it: the manager's edge came entirely from stock selection in Financials; their sector allocation actually subtracted value. Two funds with identical +0.4% active return can have opposite skill profiles — attribution reveals which is repeatable.

    6. Tracking error, active share, and closet indexing¶

    Definitions

    • Tracking error (TE) — the standard deviation of the fund's active return (fund − benchmark). Low TE = hugs the index; high TE = bold deviations. (TE is the denominator of the Information Ratio, M9.)
    • Active share — the fraction of holdings that differ from the benchmark: 12Σ|wp - wb|. 0% = the index; 100% = nothing in common.

    Closet indexing — paying active fees for an index

    A fund with low active share (e.g. <50%) but a full active TER is a closet indexer: it barely deviates from the benchmark yet charges for active management. You are paying ~1% (M4) for what an index fund delivers for ~0.2%. High active share doesn't guarantee skill, but low active share guarantees you're overpaying for whatever skill exists.

    7. The factor lens — how much "alpha" is really factor exposure?¶

    Decades of research (Fama-French and beyond) show that much of what looks like alpha is actually systematic exposure to factors:

    Factor Tilt that earns it
    Size (SMB) smaller companies
    Value (HML) cheap (low P/B, P/E) stocks
    Momentum recent winners
    Quality high-ROE, low-debt, stable earnings
    Low volatility less-volatile stocks

    The humbling insight

    A small-cap value fund that "beats the Nifty" may have zero true alpha — its excess return is the size and value factors, not stock-picking skill. A multi-factor regression (adding size/value/momentum terms to CAPM) strips these out; the intercept that survives is the real alpha. Most of it usually disappears. This is why factor/smart-beta index funds can capture cheaply what active managers charge for — a central theme of M15–M16.

    8. Common mistakes & Do's and Don'ts¶

    Analytical traps

    1. Reading alpha without R². Low R² → unreliable alpha.
    2. Crediting alpha that is really beta or factor tilt. Strip market and factors first.
    3. Wrong benchmark. Alpha vs an ill-fitting index (sector fund vs broad market) is meaningless.
    4. Paying for closet indexing. Low active share + active fee = overpaying.
    5. Confusing allocation luck with selection skill — use attribution.

    Do

    • Do report alpha with its R², beta and the correct benchmark.
    • Do run attribution to see if skill is in selection (repeatable) or allocation (often luck).
    • Do check active share before paying active fees; consider factor funds for cheap factor exposure.

    Don't

    • Don't call factor exposure "alpha".
    • Don't trust a single-period alpha — verify persistence (rolling, M9).

    10. Key takeaways¶

    Key takeaways

    • Beta = market sensitivity (regression slope); alpha = return beyond CAPM (intercept) = skill residue.
    • Read alpha with R² — low R² makes alpha unreliable.
    • Attribution splits outperformance into allocation vs selection — only some of it is repeatable skill.
    • Low active share + active fee = closet indexing — you're overpaying.
    • Much "alpha" is factor exposure (size/value/momentum/quality/low-vol); a multi-factor check often dissolves it.

    11. A word from the field¶

    On the arithmetic of skill

    "Properly measured, the average actively managed dollar must underperform the average passively managed dollar, net of costs."

    — William F. Sharpe, "The Arithmetic of Active Management" (1991); Nobel laureate and namesake of the Sharpe ratio. Alpha is a zero-sum game before costs and negative after them — which is why isolating genuine, persistent alpha (not beta or factor tilt dressed up as skill) is so hard, and so valuable.
