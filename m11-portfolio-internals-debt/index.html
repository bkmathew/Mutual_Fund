    M11 · Portfolio Internals & Debt Analytics¶

    Learning objectives

    By the end of this module you will be able to:

    • Measure an equity portfolio's internal structure — concentration (top-10, HHI), sector/cap exposure, overlap between funds, and turnover cost.
    • Define YTM, Macaulay and modified duration, and use the price-yield relation to predict a debt fund's NAV move when rates change.
    • Explain convexity, credit spreads, rating migration, and the PRC matrix in depth.
    • Recognise structural risks — liquidity / stress testing, AT1 / perpetual bonds, and side-pocketing.

    Builds on M7 (where these numbers appear on a factsheet) and M9–M10 (risk and beta). This module opens the hood: a fund's behaviour is determined by its internals, so reading them is how you anticipate risk before it shows up in returns.

    1. Intuition first — the return is in the holdings¶

    A fund is not a black box; it is a specific portfolio of securities, and every risk it carries is visible inside that portfolio if you know what to measure. For equity, the questions are: how concentrated, how exposed, how much trading? For debt, two forces dominate everything: interest-rate risk (duration) and credit risk (will issuers pay?). Master those two and you can read any debt fund.

    2. Equity internals¶

    2.1 Concentration — top-10 and the HHI¶

    Beyond the top-10 weight (M7), the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) captures concentration in one number:

    Formula — HHI (concentration)

    $\(\text{HHI} = \sum_i w_i^2 \quad(\text{weights as decimals})\)$ Higher = more concentrated. An equal-weighted 50-stock fund has HHI ≈ 0.02; a fund with a few dominant bets scores far higher. \(1/\text{HHI}\) gives the effective number of stocks.

    Worked example 1 — two funds, same count, different concentration¶

    Both hold 20 stocks. Fund P is equal-weighted (5% each): HHI = 20 × 0.05² = 0.05 → effective stocks = 1/0.05 = 20. Fund Q has 4 stocks at 15% and 16 at 2.5%: HHI = 4(0.15²) + 16(0.025²) = 0.09 + 0.01 = 0.10 → effective stocks = 10. Same 20 holdings, but Q is twice as concentrated — its result will hinge on four names. The count alone lied; HHI told the truth.

    2.2 Overlap between funds¶

    Holding three "different" funds that own the same stocks is false diversification (recall the 2026 50%-overlap rule, M3).

    Formula — portfolio overlap

    $\(\text{Overlap} = \sum_i \min(w_i^{A}, w_i^{B})\)$ summed over common holdings. 70% overlap means the two funds are 70% the same portfolio.

    2.3 Turnover — the hidden cost of churn¶

    Portfolio turnover (M7) is how much the manager traded. High turnover means more brokerage and impact cost (now capped at 6 bps cash / 2 bps derivatives, M4) plus more taxable churn — a drag that must be justified by alpha. High turnover with no outperformance is pure leakage.

    3. Debt analytics — the two forces¶

    3.1 Yield to Maturity (YTM)¶

    YTM is the annualised return if every holding is held to maturity and all coupons are reinvested at that yield — a gross indicator of the portfolio's earning power (before TER and defaults). A higher YTM is not free: it usually signals longer duration or weaker credit.

    3.2 Duration — interest-rate sensitivity¶

    Formulas — Macaulay & modified duration

    Macaulay duration = the cash-flow-weighted average time (years) to receive a bond's payments: $\(D_{\text{Mac}} = \frac{\sum_t t \cdot \frac{CF_t}{(1+y)^t}}{\text{Price}}\)$ Modified duration converts it into price sensitivity: $\(D_{\text{mod}} = \frac{D_{\text{Mac}}}{1 + y/n}\qquad\text{and}\qquad \frac{\Delta P}{P} \approx -\,D_{\text{mod}} \times \Delta y\)$ where n = compounding periods/year. A modified duration of 4 means +1% in yields → ~−4% in price.

    Worked example 2 — what a rate move does to a debt fund's NAV¶

    A debt fund reports modified duration 3.5. The RBI surprises with a +0.50% rate move (yields rise): $\(\frac{\Delta \text{NAV}}{\text{NAV}} \approx -3.5 \times 0.50\% = -1.75\%\)$ The NAV falls ~1.75% — overnight, with no default involved. A long-duration gilt (duration 8) would fall ~4%, while an overnight fund (duration ~0) barely moves. Duration is the single most important number for predicting a debt fund's volatility.

    3.3 Convexity — why duration under-states the truth¶

    Duration is a straight-line estimate, but the real price-yield relationship is curved (convex):

    The blue curve is the actual bond price as yields change; the red dashed line is the duration (tangent) estimate. Because the curve lies above the tangent on both sides, prices fall less and rise more than duration alone predicts — that gap is convexity, and it works in the bondholder's favour. Duration is accurate for small moves; for large moves you must add the convexity correction. Illustrative 10-yr 7% bond.

    3.4 Credit risk — spread, migration, default¶

    Definitions — credit

    • Credit spread — the extra yield over a government bond of equal maturity, compensating for default risk. Wider spread = market sees more risk.
    • Rating migration — a holding's rating moving (e.g. AA → A, or a downgrade to default). A downgrade widens its spread and cuts its price even before any default.
    • Accrual vs duration strategy — accrual funds earn from coupons/credit (hold to maturity, take credit risk); duration funds bet on rate moves. Reading which a fund does tells you its real risk.

    4. The PRC matrix, in depth¶

    The Potential Risk Class matrix caps how much risk a debt scheme may take, on two axes:

    Credit Risk I
    (CRV ≥ 12, safest)    		 Credit Risk II
    (CRV ≥ 10)    		 Credit Risk III
    (CRV < 10)
    Class A — Mac duration ≤ 1 yr A-I A-II A-III
    Class B — Mac duration ≤ 3 yr B-I B-II B-III
    Class C — Mac duration > 3 yr (any) C-I C-II C-III

    A scheme commits to a cell; A-I is the safest (short duration, highest credit), C-III the riskiest. The matrix lets you compare debt funds' maximum permitted risk at a glance, independent of marketing labels.

    5. Structural risks you must know¶

    Three debt-fund landmines

    1. Liquidity risk & stress testing. In a rush of redemptions a fund may be unable to sell illiquid paper without crashing its price. SEBI mandates stress testing and minimum liquid-asset holdings for open-ended debt funds, and liquidity stress-test disclosure for mid/small-cap equity funds (days to liquidate 25%/50% of the portfolio).
    2. AT1 / perpetual bonds. Additional Tier-1 bank bonds have no maturity and can be written down to zero in a crisis (as Indian investors learned in the 2020 Yes Bank episode). SEBI tightened their valuation and capped fund exposure. Treat high AT1 holdings as an equity-like risk hiding in a debt fund.
    3. Side-pocketing (segregated portfolios). When a holding defaults, the AMC may carve it into a separate "side pocket" so the main NAV reflects only good assets; you keep a claim on any future recovery. It protects fresh investors from a stale bad asset — but its presence signals the fund took credit risk that soured.

    6. Common mistakes & Do's and Don'ts¶

    Internals traps

    1. Judging diversification by stock count, not HHI/overlap. Twenty stocks can be as concentrated as ten.
    2. Chasing YTM. A high YTM usually means longer duration or weaker credit, not free return.
    3. Ignoring duration before a rate move. Duration is the volatility forecast.
    4. Assuming "debt = safe". AT1 write-downs and defaults can hit hard.
    5. Overlooking turnover cost when it isn't earning alpha.

    Do

    • Do read HHI/overlap for true diversification, and duration + PRC + credit profile for debt risk.
    • Do match a debt fund's duration to your horizon (short duration for short horizons).
    • Do check AT1 exposure, liquidity/stress disclosure, and any side pocket.

    Don't

    • Don't equate a high YTM with a better fund.
    • Don't hold multiple high-overlap funds and call it diversified.

    8. Key takeaways¶

    Key takeaways

    • A fund's risk lives in its internals — read them before the returns surprise you.
    • Equity: use HHI and overlap (not stock count) for real concentration/diversification; watch turnover cost.
    • Debt is governed by duration (rate risk: ΔP ≈ −ModDur × Δy) and credit (spread, migration, default); convexity improves on duration for big moves.
    • The PRC matrix caps a debt fund's permitted duration × credit risk — compare funds by their cell.
    • Know the landmines: liquidity/stress, AT1 write-downs, side-pocketing.

    9. A word from the field¶

    On reaching for yield

    "More money has been lost reaching for yield than at the point of a gun."

    — Raymond DeVoe Jr., market strategist. A debt fund's higher YTM is almost always paid for in duration or credit risk. Reading the internals — duration, PRC, credit profile, AT1 and liquidity — is how you tell a fair yield from a dangerous one.
