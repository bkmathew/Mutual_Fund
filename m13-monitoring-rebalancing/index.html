    M13 · Monitoring & Rebalancing¶

    Learning objectives

    By the end of this module you will be able to:

    • Separate monitoring (a periodic review) from tinkering, and know what to watch and what to ignore.
    • Apply the three rebalancing methods — calendar, threshold (tolerance-band), and hybrid — and compute the trade a drift requires.
    • Rebalance tax- and cost-efficiently using new cash flows, STP/SWP and the ₹1.25 lakh exemption.
    • Run a goal glide path, and decide when monitoring should trigger a switch versus patience.

    This is the quantitative version of M6's "how long to stay / when to shift". It uses risk metrics (M9–M11), valuation (M12), and tax (M8), and sets up the exit calculus of M14.

    1. Intuition first — rebalancing controls risk, it doesn't chase return¶

    Left alone, a portfolio drifts. A long equity rally quietly turns a balanced 60/40 portfolio into a racy 70/30 one — so just when the market is most expensive (M12), your risk is highest. Rebalancing sells what has grown and buys what has lagged, dragging the portfolio back to its chosen risk. It is systematic sell-high, buy-low, executed by a rule rather than a feeling. Crucially, its main job is risk control, not return maximisation: in a one-way bull market, rebalancing slightly lowers return (you trimmed the winner) — but it stops you carrying risk you never signed up for into the next crash.

    2. Monitoring — what to watch, what to ignore¶

    Monitor (quarterly / semi-annually)

    • Asset-allocation drift — has equity/debt/gold moved beyond your bands?
    • Mandate drift / true-to-label — is the fund still doing what its category says (M3, M7)?
    • Manager or strategy change — a key-person change weakens the track record's relevance.
    • Persistent under-performance vs category (not one quarter) and rising costs.
    • Risk metrics — has σ, drawdown, duration or credit quality crept up (M9–M11)?

    Ignore (it's noise)

    • Daily NAV and one-week/one-month moves.
    • A single weak quarter from an otherwise sound fund.
    • Headlines and tips. Over-monitoring causes over-trading.

    The cadence matters: review on a schedule, act only on a rule. Looking daily tempts you to act; reviewing quarterly lets signal separate from noise.

    3. The three rebalancing methods¶

    Definitions — rebalancing methods

    • Calendar — rebalance on a fixed date (e.g. once a year), regardless of drift. Simple, disciplined, but blind to big mid-period moves.
    • Threshold (tolerance band) — rebalance only when an asset drifts beyond a band (e.g. target 60% ± 5 → act if equity < 55% or > 65%). Responsive to markets; ignores small wiggles.
    • Hybrid (calendar + threshold) — check on a schedule, but trade only if a band is breached. The professional default: disciplined and efficient.

    Target equity 60% with a ±5% band (the green zone). The portfolio's equity weight drifts up as markets rise; each time it breaches 65%, a rebalance sells equity back to 60%. Between breaches you do nothing — avoiding needless cost. Illustrative.

    4. The drift math¶

    Formulas — drift and the rebalancing trade

    • Current weight \(w = \dfrac{\text{asset value}}{\text{total portfolio}}\)
    • Drift = current weight − target weight; act if \(|\text{drift}| >\) band.
    • Trade to restore target = (current weight − target weight) × total portfolio.

    Worked example 1 — a threshold rebalance¶

    Target 60/40 on a portfolio that has grown to ₹13,20,000, now ₹8,71,200 equity / ₹4,48,800 debt → equity weight = 8,71,200 ÷ 13,20,000 = 66.0%. That breaches the 65% upper band, so rebalance to 60%:

    \[\text{Target equity} = 0.60 \times 13{,}20{,}000 = ₹7{,}92{,}000$$ $$\text{Sell equity} = 8{,}71{,}200 - 7{,}92{,}000 = ₹79{,}200 \rightarrow \text{into debt}\]

    You have just sold equity high and bought debt low, restoring 60/40 — with no forecast, only a rule.

    5. Rebalancing without bleeding cost and tax¶

    A naïve rebalance sells, triggering exit load + capital-gains tax (M8) and the friction warned of in M6. Smarter methods cut that to near zero:

    The tax-and-cost-aware toolkit

    1. Rebalance with new cash flows first. Redirect upcoming SIP money into the under-weight asset instead of selling the over-weight one — zero tax, zero load.
    2. Use STP/SWP to move gradually rather than one taxable lump.
    3. Harvest the ₹1.25 lakh LTCG exemption when you must sell equity, and avoid selling within 12 months (20% STCG).
    4. Widen bands for high-tax/high-cost assets — a tighter band on cheap, tax-light assets; a looser one where each trade is expensive.
    5. Prefer hybrid/BAF funds (M3, M12) that rebalance internally with no investor-level tax event.

    Worked example 2 — cash-flow rebalancing beats selling¶

    Your equity sleeve is ₹50,000 over target and you run a ₹25,000/month SIP. Rather than sell ₹50,000 of equity (realising gains + maybe load), you divert the next two months' SIP (₹50,000) entirely to debt. The allocation is restored with no redemption, no tax, no load — the over-weight is corrected by where you add money, not by what you sell. For accumulating investors this handles most rebalancing for free.

    6. The goal glide path¶

    As a goal nears, monitoring shifts from "maintain 60/40" to "reduce risk on a schedule". You pre-commit a glide path that lowers equity each year approaching the goal — the disciplined version of M6's "glide to safety", and exactly what Life Cycle Funds (M3) automate.

    Worked example 3 — a 5-year glide¶

    For a goal 5 years out you might step equity down 70% → 55% → 40% → 25% → 10% over the five years, moving each tranche via STP and timing sales to use the LTCG exemption. By the goal year the corpus is mostly short-duration debt and certain — a late crash can no longer derail a needed outcome.

    7. Monitoring → action: switch or be patient?¶

    flowchart TD

    Q1 -- "Yes" --> RB["Rebalance (cash-flow first)"]
    Q1 -- "No" --> Q2{"Mandate drift / manager change /<br/>persistent vs-category underperformance?"}
    Q2 -- "Yes, structural" --> SW["Switch fund (count load + tax, M8/M14)"]
    Q2 -- "No — just a weak quarter" --> P["Be patient · do nothing"]

    The discipline: most reviews should end in "do nothing". Action is reserved for a band breach (rebalance) or a structural problem (switch) — never for a single disappointing quarter.

    8. Common mistakes & Do's and Don'ts¶

    Rebalancing traps

    1. Never rebalancing — risk silently creeps up; you ride a 70/30 into a crash you meant to avoid at 60/40.
    2. Over-rebalancing — trading on every wiggle burns cost and tax for nothing.
    3. Rebalancing by selling when a cash-flow tweak would do — needless tax.
    4. Calendar-only through a violent year — ignoring a mid-year band breach.
    5. Confusing a weak quarter with a broken fund — switching on noise.

    Do

    • Do use hybrid (calendar + threshold) rebalancing with sensible bands.
    • Do rebalance with new cash flows / STP / the LTCG exemption first.
    • Do run a pre-committed glide path as goals near.

    Don't

    • Don't monitor daily or act on single-quarter noise.
    • Don't sell to rebalance if redirecting contributions achieves the same.

    9. Applicable SEBI (Mutual Funds) Regulations, 2026¶

    • Periodic portfolio & risk disclosure — the monthly/fortnightly holdings and riskometer that enable monitoring of drift and risk creep (M7, M11). [verify]
    • True-to-label / categorisation — the legal basis for a mandate-drift switch trigger (M3). [verify]
    • Life Cycle Funds — the 2026 glide-path category automating the goal glide. [verify]
    • Dynamic Asset Allocation / BAF — vehicles that rebalance internally, tax-free to the investor. [verify]
    • Rebalancing itself is portfolio practice; SEBI mandates the disclosures and products that support it.

    10. Key takeaways¶

    Key takeaways

    • Rebalancing controls risk (systematic sell-high/buy-low); it is not a return-maximiser.
    • Monitor on a schedule, act on a rule — watch allocation drift, mandate drift, manager change and risk creep; ignore daily NAV and single quarters.
    • Use hybrid (calendar + threshold) rebalancing; compute the trade from the drift.
    • Rebalance cheaply — new cash flows first, then STP/SWP and the ₹1.25 lakh exemption; widen bands where tax/cost is high.
    • Run a pre-committed glide path near goals; most reviews should end in "do nothing".

    11. A word from the field¶

    On the real adversary

    "The investor's chief problem — and even his worst enemy — is likely to be himself."

    — Benjamin Graham, The Intelligent Investor. Monitoring and rebalancing are, at heart, rules that protect you from yourself — they pre-decide the sell-high/buy-low and reduce-risk-near-goals actions so that fear and greed don't get a vote.
