    M14 · Tax-Aware Switch & Exit¶

    Learning objectives

    By the end of this module you will be able to:

    • Compute the all-in cost of a switch or exit — exit load + STCG/LTCG (with the ₹1.25 lakh exemption) — under FIFO.
    • Apply a break-even framework to decide whether a better fund is better by enough to overcome the switching cost.
    • Use tax-loss harvesting and gain harvesting correctly, including set-off and carry-forward rules.
    • Build a tax-efficient SWP, evaluate a regular→direct switch, and apply grandfathering to old equity holdings.

    This closes Level 3. It fuses M8 (tax) with M6/M13 (switch/exit decisions and rebalancing), turning "should I move?" into a number.

    Information, not tax advice. Figures illustrative; rates current to mid-2026 (see M8).

    1. Intuition first — the bar is not "better", it's "better by enough"¶

    Every switch and every exit is a redemption — it realises tax and may trigger an exit load. So the right question is never "is Fund B better than Fund A?" It is: "is B better by enough to repay the tax and load I pay to get there?" A fund that beats yours by 0.3% a year rarely justifies a tax hit that eats two years of that edge. Tax-aware investing is mostly the discipline of not moving — and moving precisely when the math says so.

    2. The mechanics: FIFO, load, and the gain¶

    Definition — FIFO

    Mutual-fund redemptions follow First-In-First-Out: the oldest units are sold first. This determines both the holding period (hence STCG vs LTCG) and the cost base of what you redeem — critical when you've invested via SIP over many dates.

    The all-in cost of redeeming has up to two parts:

    \[\text{Switch/exit cost} = \underbrace{\text{exit load}}_{\text{if within load period}} + \underbrace{\text{capital-gains tax}}_{\text{STCG 20\% or LTCG 12.5\% over ₹1.25L (equity)}}\]

    Worked example 1 — what a switch actually costs¶

    You hold ₹10,00,000 in equity Fund A (all units >12 months, so long-term), with an embedded gain of ₹2,00,000 and no exit load remaining.

    \[\text{Taxable LTCG} = 2{,}00{,}000 - 1{,}25{,}000 = 75{,}000 \Rightarrow \text{tax} = 75{,}000 \times 12.5\% = ₹9{,}375\]

    So switching to Fund B costs ₹9,375 today (plus you re-enter B at its current NAV). That ₹9,375 is the hurdle B must clear.

    3. The switch break-even framework¶

    Formula — switch break-even

    Switching pays off only once the compounding benefit of the better fund exceeds the one-time cost: $\(\text{Corpus}\times\big[(1+r_B)^t-(1+r_A)^t\big] \;>\; \text{exit load} + \text{tax}\)$ Solve for the break-even years t. Below it, you lose; beyond it, you gain — if the expected edge \(r_B-r_A\) is real.

    Worked example 2 — is the switch worth it?¶

    Fund B is expected to beat Fund A by 0.5%/yr. Cost = ₹9,375 (Example 1), corpus ₹10,00,000:

    The green curve is the compounding benefit of a 0.5%/yr edge on ₹10 lakh; the red line is the ₹9,375 one-time cost. They cross at ≈1.7 years. If your conviction in B's edge is genuine and your horizon is well beyond ~2 years, switch; if not, stay.

    The catch — the edge is rarely certain

    The 0.5% "edge" is an expectation, not a fact (recall how fragile alpha is, M10). A real cost is paid for an uncertain benefit. That asymmetry is why you switch only for structural reasons (mandate drift, manager change, persistent category lag — M6/M13), not a hoped-for sliver of outperformance.

    4. Harvesting — turning the tax rules in your favour¶

    4.1 Gain harvesting (use the ₹1.25 lakh exemption every year)¶

    Because the equity LTCG exemption is per financial year, a long-term investor can redeem up to ₹1.25 lakh of long-term gains tax-free each year and immediately re-buy, resetting the cost base upward. Done annually, this quietly shelters lakhs of gains from future tax — at the cost of minor friction (re-buy NAV, any load).

    4.2 Tax-loss harvesting (offset gains with losses)¶

    Set-off rules (key ones)

    • Short-term capital loss (STCL) can offset both STCG and LTCG.
    • Long-term capital loss (LTCL) can offset only LTCG.
    • Unused losses carry forward up to 8 years (if the return is filed on time).

    Worked example 3 — harvesting a loss¶

    You have a realised ₹50,000 STCG (taxable at 20% → ₹10,000). You also hold a fund sitting on a ₹50,000 unrealised short-term loss. Redeem the loser to book ₹50,000 STCL, which offsets the STCG:

    \[\text{Tax saved} = 50{,}000 \times 20\% = ₹10{,}000\]

    You can re-enter the sold fund (or a similar one) to keep the exposure. The loss did the work the market wouldn't.

    5. The regular → direct switch — almost always worth it¶

    Switching a regular plan to its direct version is a taxable switch, but it saves the commission (~1%/yr) forever (M4). The break-even is usually fast.

    Worked example 4 — regular→direct break-even¶

    ₹10,00,000 in a regular plan; direct saves 1%/yr ≈ ₹10,000/yr. One-time switch tax (as Example 1) ≈ ₹9,375. Break-even ≈ ₹9,375 ÷ ₹10,000 ≈ 0.9 years — under a year, after which the saving is pure gain, compounding for decades. For any long horizon, moving to direct is one of the highest-return switches available — provided you don't need the adviser the commission was paying for.

    6. Tax-efficient exit via SWP¶

    For drawing income, an SWP taxes only the gain portion of each withdrawal (M8), unlike fully-taxed IDCW. Two design rules:

    1. Set a sustainable withdrawal rate (often ~4–6% of corpus/yr) so the corpus outlives you.
    2. Mind sequence-of-returns risk — heavy withdrawals during an early market fall can permanently deplete the corpus; keep 1–2 years of withdrawals in low-volatility assets (the glide-path logic of M13).

    Worked example 5 — SWP tax efficiency¶

    A ₹40,00,000 corpus with a 25% embedded gain; you withdraw ₹2,00,000 in a year. Only ~25% (₹50,000) is gain; if long-term equity, it's within the ₹1.25 lakh exemption → ₹0 tax this year. The same ₹2,00,000 taken as IDCW would be taxed at your slab. SWP is the tax-efficient way to draw a paycheck from a corpus.

    7. Grandfathering — for equity bought before 31 Jan 2018¶

    Definition — grandfathering (Sec 112A)

    For equity units acquired before 1 Feb 2018, the cost for LTCG is the higher of actual cost or the fair market value on 31 Jan 2018. Gains up to that date are exempt. If you hold very old equity funds, computing the grandfathered cost can sharply reduce the taxable gain on exit — don't overpay by using the original purchase price.

    9. Common mistakes & Do's and Don'ts¶

    Switch/exit traps

    1. Switching for a tiny expected edge that the tax/load eats for years.
    2. Ignoring FIFO and accidentally redeeming <12-month units at 20% STCG.
    3. Mis-applying set-off (e.g. using LTCL against STCG — not allowed).
    4. Taking IDCW for income when an SWP is far more tax-efficient.
    5. Forgetting grandfathering on pre-2018 equity and over-paying tax.
    6. An unsustainable SWP rate plus sequence risk depleting the corpus.

    Do

    • Do compute the break-even before any switch; move only when benefit clearly beats cost.
    • Do harvest the ₹1.25 lakh exemption yearly and offset gains with losses.
    • Do move regular→direct for the long term; draw income via SWP.

    Don't

    • Don't churn funds chasing performance.
    • Don't redeem without checking holding period (FIFO) and the load window.

    11. Key takeaways¶

    Key takeaways

    • Every switch/exit is a redemption — it realises tax + load under FIFO; the bar is "better by enough".
    • Use the break-even framework: switch only when the compounding edge beats the one-time cost over your horizon (≈1.7 yrs in the worked case).
    • Harvest yearly — the ₹1.25 lakh exemption (gain harvesting) and losses (STCL offsets STCG+LTCG; LTCL only LTCG; carry 8 yrs).
    • Regular→direct usually pays back in under a year; draw income via a tax-efficient SWP, mindful of sequence risk.
    • Apply grandfathering to pre-2018 equity to avoid over-paying.

    12. A word from the field¶

    On the value of staying put

    "Our favorite holding period is forever."

    — Warren Buffett, Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letter. The tax code rewards exactly this: every avoided redemption is tax deferred and compounding. Move only when the break-even math — not the latest performance table — tells you to.
