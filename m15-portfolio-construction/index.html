    M15 · Portfolio Construction¶

    Learning objectives

    By the end of this module you will be able to:

    • Explain why asset allocation, not fund selection, drives most of a portfolio's long-run outcome.
    • Build a portfolio with the core-satellite method and make the active/passive/factor choice deliberately.
    • Diversify across and within asset classes (equity caps, debt duration/credit, gold, international, REITs/InvITs) and size positions.
    • Choose the right number of funds, avoiding overlap, and match construction to risk capacity, tolerance and need.

    This synthesises the analyst tools (M9–M14) into building a portfolio. It opens Level 4 and feeds archetypes (M16) and the capstone (M21).

    1. Intuition first — architecture beats selection¶

    The biggest decision is not "which flexi-cap fund?" but "how much equity vs debt vs gold?" Decades of research find that the asset-allocation policy explains the large majority of the variability of a portfolio's returns over time — far more than security or fund selection. Two investors who both pick decent funds but hold 80/20 vs 40/60 equity/debt will have entirely different journeys. Get the architecture right and average funds serve you well; get it wrong and the best funds won't save you.

    2. Asset allocation first¶

    Allocation flows from three things, decided before any fund is chosen:

    The three inputs to allocation

    • Risk capacity — your ability to take risk (horizon, income stability, emergency buffer). Objective.
    • Risk tolerance — your willingness to stomach volatility. Psychological.
    • Need — the return required to meet the goal. If a modest return suffices, don't take equity risk you don't need.

    The binding constraint is the lowest of the three. A young investor with high capacity but low tolerance should not run 90% equity — they'll panic-sell (M6). Allocation is where capacity, tolerance and need are reconciled into weights.

    Worked example 1 — weighted expected return and risk¶

    A moderate ₹50,00,000 portfolio: 60% equity (exp. 12%), 30% debt (7%), 10% gold (8%).

    \[E(R_p) = 0.60(12) + 0.30(7) + 0.10(8) = 7.2 + 2.1 + 0.8 = 10.1\%\]

    Risk is less than the weighted-average volatility because the assets aren't perfectly correlated — equity, debt and gold often zig and zag at different times. That imperfect correlation is the "free lunch" of diversification: lower portfolio risk for the same return.

    3. Core-satellite construction¶

    The professional default structure:

    flowchart TD
    P["Portfolio"] --> CORE["CORE (low-cost, ~60–70%)<br/>broad index funds / large-cap"]
    P --> SAT["SATELLITES (active/tilts, ~30–40%)<br/>flexi-cap · mid/small · factor · thematic · intl/gold"]
    CORE --> Cwhy["cheap, reliable market return<br/>(M4 cost, M10 low alpha in large-caps)"]
    SAT --> Swhy["selective bets where active/factor<br/>edge may exist"]

    classDef s fill:#ede7f6,stroke:#5e35b1,color:#311b92;

    The core captures the market cheaply (index/large-cap, where active alpha is hardest — M10); the satellites take deliberate, smaller bets (mid/small-cap, factor/smart-beta, international, gold) where an edge may exist or where diversification is added. The illustrative ₹50L portfolio above puts 35% in an index core, layers active and mid/small satellites, and rounds out with debt, gold and a liquid buffer.

    4. The active / passive / factor decision¶

    A rule of thumb by market efficiency

    • Large-caps — highly researched, efficient; most active managers lag the TRI after cost (M10). Lean passive (index/ETF).
    • Mid/small-caps — less efficient; some active managers add value, but at higher cost and risk. Active can earn its fee here, selectively.
    • Factor/smart-beta — capture value/quality/momentum/low-vol cheaply via rules-based index funds, instead of paying active fees for what is really factor exposure (M10).

    The decision is not "active or passive" dogmatically, but passive where markets are efficient, active/factor where evidence of edge exists — and always weighing the cost (M4).

    5. Diversify across and within¶

    Two axes of diversification

    • Across asset classes: equity, debt, gold, international, cash — and increasingly REITs/InvITs for real-estate/infra income. Low mutual correlation is what cuts portfolio risk.
    • Within each class: equity across market caps and styles; debt across duration and credit (M11); not three funds that secretly hold the same stocks (overlap, M11).

    International equity adds geography and currency diversification (and hedges home-country risk); gold is a crisis/inflation diversifier; debt stabilises and funds rebalancing. Each earns its place by behaving differently, not by adding more of the same.

    6. How many funds? Position sizing¶

    4–6 funds is usually enough

    A complete portfolio often needs only: 1 index core + 1 flexi/large core + 1 mid-small satellite + 1–2 debt + optionally gold/international. Beyond ~6–8 funds you mostly add overlap (M11), not diversification — many "diversified" 15-fund portfolios are one closet index fund with extra paperwork and tax lots. Size satellite bets small enough that being wrong is survivable.

    Worked example 2 — more funds ≠ more diversification¶

    You hold five large-cap-heavy equity funds. Measuring overlap (M11), their top holdings are 70%+ common — effectively one large-cap exposure split five ways, with five expense ratios and five capital-gains lots to track. Replacing them with one index core + one genuine mid/small satellite increases real diversification while cutting cost and complexity.

    7. Goal-bucketing — tying construction to time¶

    A clean way to operationalise allocation is buckets by horizon (each mapped to vehicles from M3):

    Bucket Horizon Allocation
    Safety < 2 yrs / emergency Liquid, overnight, ultra-short debt
    Stability 2–5 yrs Short-duration debt, hybrids, BAF
    Growth 5+ yrs Diversified equity (core-satellite)

    Buckets prevent the cardinal error of funding a near-term goal with equity, and they make the glide path (M13) concrete: as a goal moves from Growth → Stability → Safety, you de-risk on schedule.

    8. Common mistakes & Do's and Don'ts¶

    Construction traps

    1. No asset-allocation plan — picking funds before deciding equity/debt weights.
    2. Over-diversification — 15 funds that overlap; complexity without benefit.
    3. 100% equity regardless of horizon/tolerance — guarantees panic-selling at the worst time.
    4. Recency-driven construction — loading last year's hot category.
    5. Ignoring correlation — stacking assets that move together (e.g. equity + high-yield credit + thematic) and calling it diversified.

    Do

    • Do decide asset allocation first, from capacity ∧ tolerance ∧ need.
    • Do use core-satellite: cheap index core, selective active/factor satellites.
    • Do diversify across and within, keep to 4–6 funds, and bucket by goal.

    Don't

    • Don't confuse number of funds with diversification — check overlap.
    • Don't build for last year's winners.

    9. Applicable SEBI (Mutual Funds) Regulations, 2026¶

    • Categorisation framework — the standardised categories (M3) are the building blocks construction selects among; Multi-Asset and Index/ETF categories directly enable allocation. [verify]
    • Scheme-overlap rule — the 50% cap on sectoral/thematic overlap supports genuine diversification (M3/M11). [verify]
    • True-to-label & riskometer — ensure each block behaves as labelled, so allocation weights mean what you intend. [verify]
    • PMS / AIF sit outside the MF Regulations as an adjacency for larger portfolios — the subject of M16.
    • Construction is portfolio practice; SEBI supplies the regulated building blocks.

    10. Key takeaways¶

    Key takeaways

    • Asset allocation drives most of the outcome — decide equity/debt/gold/intl weights before choosing funds.
    • Allocation = the lowest of risk capacity, tolerance and need.
    • Build core-satellite: cheap index core where markets are efficient; selective active/factor satellites where edge exists.
    • Diversify across and within; 4–6 funds usually suffice — more adds overlap, not safety.
    • Bucket by goal/horizon and de-risk along the glide path.

    11. A word from the field¶

    On the only free lunch

    "Diversification is the only free lunch in investing."

    — Harry Markowitz, Nobel laureate and father of Modern Portfolio Theory. Construction is the craft of earning that lunch: combining assets that behave differently so the whole portfolio carries less risk than the sum of its parts — which no amount of clever fund-picking can replicate.
