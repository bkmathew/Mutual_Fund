    M21 · Capstone — Synthesis, Model Portfolio & Investment Policy Statement¶

    Learning objectives

    By the end of this module you will be able to:

    • Assemble everything from M1–M20 into a written Investment Policy Statement (IPS) for a real Indian investor case.
    • Specify an illustrative model portfolio by category (not specific funds) using core-satellite construction.
    • Apply the four lifecycle decisions and tax-aware logic end-to-end, with worked numbers.
    • Place it all in a grounded mid-2026 context — without forecasting.

    Illustrative, not advice

    The case, figures, allocations and fund categories below are illustrative teaching examples — not recommendations and not personal advice. No specific scheme is named or endorsed. Real plans must be built for the individual, ideally with a SEBI-registered adviser.

    This is the final module. It proves the program by using it: turning knowledge into a plan that runs on autopilot and survives emotion.

    1. Intuition first — the IPS is the whole program, written down¶

    Everything you've learned is inert until it becomes a plan you actually follow. An Investment Policy Statement (IPS) is that plan: a short written document stating what you're investing for, how much risk you'll take, what you'll buy, when you'll rebalance, how you'll handle tax, and when you'll exit. Its real job is behavioural — it pre-decides your actions so that, in a crash or a mania, you consult a rule rather than your fear (the root cause of M19). The capstone builds one.

    2. The case (illustrative)¶

    Investor profile

    Anjali, 34, salaried, take-home ~₹1,20,000/month, stable job, 6-month emergency fund already in place. She can invest ₹40,000/month plus a ₹10,00,000 bonus. Moderate risk tolerance, long horizon, new-regime taxpayer (30% slab). Three goals:

    Goal Horizon Rough target
    House down-payment 5 years ₹50 lakh
    Child's education 12 years ₹40 lakh
    Retirement 26 years corpus build

    3. Her Investment Policy Statement¶

    3.1 Objectives & required return¶

    Three goals, three horizons (§2). Equity for the far goals (time to ride out volatility), safety for the near one (M6/M15).

    3.2 Risk capacity ∧ tolerance ∧ need (M15)¶

    High capacity (stable income, long horizon, emergency buffer), moderate tolerance → the binding constraint is tolerance, so no 100% equity; use a moderate, goal-tiered allocation.

    3.3 Asset-allocation policy — by goal bucket (M13/M15)¶

    Bucket Horizon Equity Debt Gold Vehicles (categories, M3)
    House 5 yr 30% 65% 5% BAF + short-duration debt
    Education 12 yr 65% 30% 5% Index core + flexi + short debt
    Retirement 26 yr 75% 20% 5% Index core + flexi + mid/small + gold

    Riskier for far goals, safer for near ones — the glide principle baked in from day one.

    3.4 Fund-selection criteria (M4/M7/M9/M10/M11)¶

    Every fund must pass: Direct plan (lowest cost, M4); true-to-label and right category (M3/M7); consistent rolling returns vs the TRI (not point-to-point, M7/M9); sound risk-adjusted profile (Sharpe/Sortino, M9); for active picks, evidence of alpha not just factor tilt and decent active share (M10); for debt, acceptable duration/credit/PRC (M11); and low overlap across the set (M11/M15). Core = low-cost index; satellites = selective active/factor.

    3.5 Contribution plan (M5/M6/M12)¶

    • SIP ₹40,000/month split: House ₹15k, Education ₹12k, Retirement ₹13k (auto-mandate with a higher ceiling for step-ups, M5).
    • ₹10,00,000 bonus: deploy valuation-aware (M12) — into equity buckets via a 6–18 month STP depending on the valuation band, parked in liquid meanwhile; the House portion goes mostly to short-duration debt directly.

    3.6 Rebalancing & monitoring rules (M13)¶

    Hybrid method: review quarterly, act only on a ±5% band breach; rebalance with new SIP cash flows first to avoid tax; widen bands where tax/load is high.

    3.7 Tax policy (M8/M14)¶

    Hold equity >12 months; harvest the ₹1.25 lakh LTCG exemption yearly; offset gains with losses; respect FIFO; draw any future income via SWP not IDCW; remember post-2023 debt is slab-taxed (M8).

    3.8 Exit / glide rules (M6/M13/M14)¶

    As each goal enters its final 2–3 years, glide that bucket from equity to short-duration debt. Switch a fund only for cause (mandate drift, manager change, persistent category lag) and only when the break-even math clears (M14).

    3.9 Review cadence¶

    Full IPS review annually or on a life change (income, goals, dependents).

    4. The model portfolio (illustrative categories)¶

    Category-level model (no specific schemes)

    • Core (~45%): broad index funds (Nifty 50 / Nifty 500), Direct-Growth.
    • Active satellites (~25%): a flexi-cap + a small mid/small-cap sleeve.
    • Debt (~22%): short-duration + corporate bond (high credit), matched to each goal's horizon.
    • Diversifiers (~8%): gold (and optionally a small international index sleeve).

    Kept to ~6 funds total to avoid overlap (M11/M15), all Direct plans, rebalanced by the §3.6 rule.

    5. The four decisions, end-to-end (worked)¶

    ① Enter. SIPs run automatically; the bonus is STP'd into equity over the chosen window — no market-timing (M6/M12).

    ② Stay. Horizon-matched; SIPs never paused in a crash — a fall buys the most units (M6).

    Illustrative growth of the retirement SIP (₹13,000/month, 26 years, ~11% net): FV = 13,000 × [ (1 + 0.11/12)³¹² − 1 ] ÷ (0.11/12) ≈ ₹2.3 crore The education SIP (₹12,000, 12 yr, ~10%) reaches ~₹33 lakh — short of the ₹40 lakh target, which the IPS fixes with an annual step-up (raising the SIP as income grows). Naming the shortfall and the lever is the IPS doing its job.

    ③ Switch / rebalance. Quarterly review; rebalance via new cash flows on a ±5% breach; switch funds only for cause and only past the tax/load break-even (M13/M14).

    ④ Exit. Glide each bucket to safety in its final years; draw retirement income later via a tax-efficient SWP (M14), mindful of sequence risk.

    6. Mid-2026 grounding (context, not forecast)¶

    Where things stand (mid-2026)

    • The industry is large and growing — ~₹81.6 lakh crore AUM, ~₹30,000 crore/month SIPs, ~27.7 crore folios (M17) — with the SIP book stabilising markets.
    • The SEBI (Mutual Funds) Regulations, 2026 are live (1 Apr 2026), phasing to 2029: cheaper, clearer, better-governed funds (M18).
    • Cost is falling (unbundling, passive price war) — a structural tailwind for disciplined investors (M4/M17).
    • Valuation should set deployment pace and expectations, never a market call (M12).

    No predictions here — these are conditions, and all figures move. The plan is built to be robust to whatever markets do, not to forecast them.

    The aim of the entire program: a plan so well-specified that, most of the time, the right action is to do nothing — and the few actions needed are pre-decided by rule.

    8. Common mistakes & Do's and Don'ts (program-level)¶

    Do

    • Do write the IPS and let it pre-decide your behaviour.
    • Do keep it cheap (Direct/index core), diversified (4–6, low-overlap), automated (SIP), and tax-smart.
    • Do glide near goals and review on a schedule.

    Don't

    • Don't abandon the plan in a crash or a mania.
    • Don't chase performance, over-diversify, or switch without the break-even math.

    9. Applicable SEBI (Mutual Funds) Regulations, 2026¶

    The IPS operates entirely within the 2026 framework (M18): Direct plans and TER unbundling (cost, M4); categorisation & true-to-label (the building blocks, M3); riskometer/PRC (risk matching, M7/M11); nomination, KYC, MITRA, grievance (the journey, M5); and the tax law (Sec 111A/112A/50AA, M8). A good plan is simply the disciplined use of what the law makes available. [verify specific provisions per linked module]

    10. Key takeaways — the whole program in five lines¶

    Capstone takeaways

    • Write an IPS — it converts knowledge into rule-based behaviour that survives emotion.
    • Allocation first, by goal bucket; riskier for far goals, safer for near ones.
    • Keep it cheap, diversified, automated, true-to-label and tax-smart — and glide near goals.
    • Most of the time, do nothing; act only on a band breach or a cause that clears the break-even.
    • The market you can't control; cost, behaviour, diversification and tax you can — and that is where the program's edge lies.

    11. A word from the field — and a close¶

    On the simple discipline that wins

    "The big money is not in the buying and the selling, but in the waiting."

    — Charlie Munger. Across twenty-one modules the lesson converges on this: define the plan, automate the contributions, control cost and tax, diversify sensibly, and then have the patience to let compounding work uninterrupted. The investor who does the ordinary things consistently — and resists the urge to be clever — quietly beats most of those who try to be brilliant.
